Case study 01 · Fortune 500 technology company
Prioritizing the employee experience at scale
Quantitative

A global technology corporation needed to choose where to invest across its internal employee platforms. Every journey looked important; leadership lacked a defensible, data-driven way to prioritize. I was engaged as an expert quantitative UX researcher to rank 25 employee journeys and identify what actually drives satisfaction.

Method
  • MaxDiff (best-worst scaling) to force the trade-offs that rating scales hide; fielded to ~5,900 U.S. employees, ~600 completes in one week (95% CI, ±4% margin of error).
  • Balanced Incomplete Block Design generated in R for statistically fair choice sets; each respondent judged 10 to 12 randomized sets of five journeys.
  • Hierarchical Bayes modeling estimated utility scores per journey, per respondent, surfacing preference structure that averages flatten. Tools: Qualtrics; R (bayesm, support.BWS2, ggplot2).

Case study 02 · AI startup · highly regulated industry
Designing trustworthy AI for regulated workflows
Research → architecture

A startup set out to automate five expert-grade report types in real estate development: documents traditionally hand-built by consultants over months, in a highly regulated domain. As Lead AI & UX Research Strategist, I ran an eighteen-week research program where research didn't just inform the product; it became the AI system's architecture.

Research questions
  • RQ1What makes an AI-generated report trustworthy?
  • RQ2Where does generalized AI fail in regulated environments?
  • RQ3How can UX research drive prompt-system architecture?
1Qualitative discovery 2Process modeling 3Quantitative validation 4Prompt engineering 5Design integration
Phases 1–2 · Listening before building
  • 12 subject-matter-expert interviews (urban planning, marketing, energy, finance) analyzed with a rapid mixed-matrix method, plus 14 expert reports annotated for structure, logic, and tone to reverse-engineer what "good" looked like.
  • Modeled all five report workflows in BPMN, identifying 25 to 35 subprocesses per report and validating each with experts; the result was a modular, AI-friendly architecture.
Headline finding

Factual accuracy alone does not create trust. Logic clarity, source transparency, regulation visibility, and professional tone alignment do.

Phases 4–5 · Research became architecture
Design as prompt system
  • Parameterized prompts built directly from the BPMN models.
  • Function calling and RAG integrated for consistency and grounding.
  • Partnered with frontend and backend engineering on implementation.
Human experts in the loop
  • SME walkthroughs scored outputs on clarity, logic, tone, and compliance rubrics.
  • Structured surveys plus interviews fed every iteration.
  • Annotated prompt logic delivered straight to engineers.
  • RQ1 answered: trust = explainability, citation, context → prompt logic redesigned to display assumptions and cite sources prominently.
  • RQ2 answered: generalized AI fails on regulation → region-tuned logic and formula-level integration replaced generic generation.
  • RQ3 answered: the prompt workflow itself became the product specification. Report automation reached 100% completion on three of five report types during my engagement.
Case study 03 · Meta · Developer Experience
Scaling qualitative insight for the developer experience
Qual at scale

Meta's quarterly Developer Experience survey guides strategic investment in internal tools: 13 to 15 open-ended questions, 5,000 to 10,000 responses per question, analyzed by 30+ partner teams with no shared standards. The result was inconsistent insight that was hard to present cohesively to leadership. I designed a four-project program that turned scattered analysis into one harmonized system.

The program
3.1 · Baseline a codebook · 4 wks
  • Engineers describe the same tool many ways (VSCODE, VS Code, vsc, typos). Abductive coding with expert validation produced 300+ standardized keywords, socialized until the codebook became the shared standard.
3.2 · Thematic analysis at scale · 4 wks
  • Full-lifecycle thematic analysis turning thousands of responses into themes, proportions, and exemplars; a broad readout validated quant trends with qual evidence and opened new Data Science collaborations.
3.3 · Cognitive testing framework · 2 wks
  • A repeatable five-phase framework (think-aloud interviews, matrix analysis, revision) built to a two-week cadence; adopted by multiple teams and extended to user testing.
3.4 · AI-assisted analysis · 2 wks
  • Triangulated evaluation of manual coding vs. LDA topic modeling vs. an LLM workflow against human-labeled benchmarks, judged on accuracy, efficiency, and fit-for-purpose.
The automation verdict
  • Manual coding: gold-standard validity, but ~90 minutes per 100 responses; untenable at survey scale.
  • Topic modeling (LDA): fast, but keyword clusters miss granular, tool-specific pain points and can't answer stakeholder questions interactively.
  • LLM workflow (recommended): summarized 117 responses (6,000 words) into themes in minutes, reached 84% classification agreement with human annotators, and surfaced emerging themes; it informed productionization of AI-assisted thematic analysis, and the system later served as a training signal for an internal model.

Case study 04 · Bellefolio · founder-led research
Building an AI marketplace from the user inward
Generative + evaluative

Bellefolio is an AI-enabled marketplace I conceived, researched, and built from zero to a working product. With no team to delegate to, I ran the full research loop myself, generative discovery through evaluative testing, and translated every finding directly into the product and its underlying AI behavior. It's the clearest demonstration of my thesis: research that sits next to the build changes what gets built.

Research questions
  • RQ1What makes users trust an AI-driven recommendation enough to act on it?
  • RQ2Where does the discovery experience break down between intent and result?
  • RQ3How should research findings translate into prompt and interaction design?
Method
  • Generative discovery: mapped the marketplace journey and the decision points where users hesitate, grounded in my published research on trust formation and buyer behavior.
  • Rapid evaluative loops: usability testing on live AI-powered discovery and personalization features, observing where recommendations earned trust and where they lost it.
  • Research-to-prompt translation: turned behavioral findings into multi-turn prompt frameworks and interaction patterns, then re-tested; a build-measure-learn loop validating product assumptions against real use.
Carried-over insight

The trust drivers I documented in regulated AI held in a consumer context too: users extend trust when the system shows its reasoning and gives them control over the input, not when it simply asserts a confident answer.
