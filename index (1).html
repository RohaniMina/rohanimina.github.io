01 — How I think about positioning
Narrative that survives contact with technical buyers
Most AI positioning oversells what the model does and underserves the buyer's actual decision. My approach is the opposite, and it's consistent across every project below.
01
Research the buyer
Positioning starts with evidence about how the buyer actually decides, not with a feature list or a competitor's deck.
02
Respect the technology
I work close enough to the product to make claims that hold up; messaging that outruns the model loses technical audiences fast.
03
One narrative, many rooms
Engineers, executives, and investors each need a different cut of the same story, calibrated to their decision criteria, never contradicting each other.
04
Enable, don't just launch
A launch is the start. I build the collateral, demos, and enablement that let others tell the story without me in the room.

Project 01 · Bellefolio · founder & product marketing lead
I lived the problem, validated it, named it, then shipped it
0→1 GTM · Live

Bellefolio is the clearest proof of how I work: I found a real problem, validated it rigorously, turned the insight into a narrative, and then built the product to back the narrative up. Every platform I tried showed overall ratings for a salon, never expertise by service; I needed someone exceptional at balayage, not four stars for everything. That gap became the positioning, and the positioning became a shipped AI product.

"Is this just me, or is there a real gap in the market?"
Buyer & market validation
  • 15+ customer discovery interviews with clients and independent stylists, mapping how each side makes and evaluates a match.
  • Competitive audit of Yelp, StyleSeat, Booksy, and Vagaro; I mapped exactly where each existing solution breaks down and why venue-level ratings fail a service-level decision.
  • The core insight that became the messaging: clients don't want to browse, they want to be matched; stylists don't want a directory listing, they want a portfolio that grows with their career.
The positioning

The market needed a service-specific matching layer, not another booking aggregator. That one sentence drove the product, the messaging, and the GTM.

  • Pricing, packaging, and commercialization decisions for a two-sided marketplace.

Project 02 · Aktus AI · lead AI product marketing
GTM positioning for an agentic AI platform
Positioning · Enablement

At Aktus AI, I owned end-to-end go-to-market positioning for a platform automating expert-grade development reports for an enterprise client in a highly regulated industry, documents that traditionally took hundreds of consultants and months of work. The challenge was a skeptical, technical buyer who had every reason not to trust AI output. So I built the positioning from evidence, not adjectives.

What I did
  • Buyer & competitive research: deep market and customer research across 8+ report types, benchmarking outputs against top consulting firms to define differentiated positioning and find the gaps.
  • Translated capability into value: turned complex LLM and RAG behavior into value propositions mapped to the buyer's real decision criteria, calibrated for a technical audience.
  • Kept claims honest: partnered with the RAG engineering team on grounding and hallucination analysis so every GTM claim matched actual product performance.
  • Built the sales enablement layer: collateral, demos, and product documentation that let non-technical stakeholders champion the platform internally and move deals forward.
The insight that anchored the narrative

From 12 expert interviews and 14 annotated reports: factual accuracy alone does not create trust. Logic clarity, source transparency, and tone alignment do. That reshaped the product, the prompt architecture, and the sales story together.

Project 04 · Meta · developer experience research
How engineering teams actually adopt developer tools
Adoption insight

The best developer-tool positioning comes from understanding why developers adopt or abandon tools. Embedded in Meta's Developer Experience organization for over a year, I studied exactly that, across the company-wide DevEx survey and a portfolio of tool-specific studies, the raw material behind any credible developer-marketing narrative.

What I did
  • Owned qualitative analysis for Meta's company-wide quarterly DevEx survey: 13–15 open-ended questions, 7,000–10,000 highly technical responses each, spanning the full development lifecycle.
  • Replaced scattered analysis across 30+ partner teams with a single harmonized system: I built a codebook and analysis playbook that standardized how thousands of responses became cohesive, prioritized insight for leadership.
  • Led 9+ mixed-methods studies on developer tools and AI-assisted workflows; surfaced adoption barriers and competitive friction points that informed roadmap and positioning.
  • Co-developed an AI-assisted analysis approach built on my codebook (evaluating topic modeling, then an internal LLM) that scaled insight generation; later used as a training signal for an internal model.
Why it matters for PMM

I can write developer messaging grounded in how engineers actually evaluate tools, what earns adoption and what kills it, because I spent a year measuring it inside one of the largest engineering orgs in the world.

Project 05 · Meta · AI-assisted developer tools (Metamate)
Positioning an AI coding assistant inside the developer workflow
Product positioning · VoC

An AI coding assistant only wins if it meets developers where they already work. I led the research that answered the core positioning question for Meta's AI-assisted developer tool (Metamate): where, in the real coding workflow, does this tool earn its place? The output wasn't just usability findings; it was the evidence for how to strategically position the product inside the development process.

What I did
  • Video ethnography with Meta software engineers (45–60 minute sessions in their real work environments) to see how they actually use and interact with AI-assisted tools, then mapped the contextual factors driving their behavior.
  • Built a journey framework and flowchart of the developer's path through VS Code, pinpointing pain points and the exact moments where an AI assistant could integrate and add value.
  • Ran cognitive testing on the tool's sentiment measurement and in-depth interviews on its response-feedback experience, turning both into concrete product and positioning recommendations.
Why it matters for PMM

This is voice-of-customer research aimed squarely at product positioning: I identified the workflow moments where the tool was most valuable, which is exactly the input a positioning and messaging strategy for a developer AI product is built on.

Project 06 · Meta · pre-launch validation (Teleport)
De-risking a product launch before it shipped
Launch readiness

A launch is only as strong as the confidence behind it. Before Meta shipped Teleport, a backend telemetry tool for Windows, I led the cross-functional research that validated it actually worked and didn't degrade the user experience, the launch-readiness evidence a go-to-market decision rests on.

What I did
  • Designed a quasi-experimental study with qualitative analysis: participants recorded 10–15 minutes of real daily work so I could validate the tool's telemetry against six actual user behaviors and surface any pain points across their full toolset.
  • Ran a batched rollout (three cohorts), using the first cohort to resolve installation and reliability issues before scaling, minimizing participant friction and protecting the timeline.
  • Acted as the liaison between engineering and recruiting, coordinating installs and mapping log data to observed behavior to confirm the tool captured activity correctly.

05 — About
Translation is the throughline

The throughline of my career is translation. As a professor, I translated analytics and consumer behavior for audiences from undergraduates to executives. As a researcher inside Meta and Google, I translated what engineers said into what product teams built. As a product marketer, I translate what AI systems do into reasons people trust them.

I hold a PhD in Marketing and Consumer Behavior from HEC Montréal, followed by two years of postdoctoral research in service marketing and consumer behavior, where my work centered on trust formation and adoption, the same questions I now work on with AI products. I then served as tenure-track faculty at Saint Mary's College of California and the University of Saskatchewan, carrying a full academic load of original research, teaching, and institutional service, and publishing peer-reviewed work along the way. I came to marketing from an engineering education, which is probably why technical products feel like home.

These days I split my time between building Bellefolio, advising early-stage AI companies, and studying how the newest generation of AI development tools is changing who gets to build software, and how we tell that story.
